Moving On is Not Easy, But It Is Possible: 10 Actionable Steps

The pain of lost love is common irrespective of reason—whether due to external circumstances, family rejection, or merely drifting apart. This article looks at actionable recommendations and culturally sensitive means to improve and move on.

Learning About Heartbreak

Relationships are not just between two people but often between families, society, and expectations in collectivist cultures such as Indian culture. In addition to losing your other half, the end of a relationship can also mean giving up on future hopes and dreams—such as time with family during holidays and a future you have constructed with great care.

This makes heartbreak so convoluted. With each encounter between two or more individuals, their culture comes into play. There is pressure to rush forward quickly, particularly from older family members who may suggest an arranged marriage or another union as a solution; the process of healing then feels hurried and invalidating.

Remember that there is no right or wrong way to release a loved one. Healing comes at its own pace.

Things to Do

1. Stop Blaming Yourself

Don't blame yourself for your current struggles. Accepting that your feelings are real and legitimate is the first step toward healing. There is a vague presumption in our culture that men must be tough and women must let go for the sake of family honor. Suppressing emotions only delays recovery.

2. Let Yourself Mourn

Let yourself mourn. If needed, weep. Feel lost, confused, angry, or depressed. None of these emotions signal weakness. They reflect the depth of the bond you shared. Talk to a counselor or trusted friends who will not judge you and can support you through the grieving process.

3. Cease Communication (Yes, Really)

This is one of the hardest yet most crucial steps. Social media platforms like Instagram and WhatsApp make staying connected effortless—and that is the problem. Seeing stories, rereading chats, or accidentally liking posts only reopens wounds.

Delete or archive old chats. Unfollow them on social media. Ask common friends not to share updates about your ex. If you work or study together, keep interactions polite but brief. Even “just checking in as friends” can delay healing. A no-contact period of at least three to six months is recommended.

4. Rediscover Yourself

Heartbreak can be an opportunity for self-discovery. When identities merge in relationships, we often lose parts of ourselves. Reconnect with your interests. Enroll in a class, go hiking, explore the Western Ghats, or reconnect with old friends.

Read Gulzar’s poetry or novels like The God of Small Things by Arundhati Roy. Watch inspiring films such as Queen (2013) or Zindagi Na Milegi Dobara (2011), which beautifully portray self-discovery and resilience.

5. Express Your Emotions Creatively

Indian culture has a rich tradition of emotional expression through poetry, music, and art. Use it. Maintain a physical journal and write freely—in Hindi, English, or any language you are comfortable with. This is a private and healing practice.

6. Take Care of Your Physical Health

Heartbreak can manifest physically through loss of appetite, fatigue, insomnia, or stress eating. Occasional indulgence is fine, but consistency matters. Exercise releases endorphins, the body’s natural mood boosters.

You don’t need an expensive gym. Practice yoga, pranayama, deep breathing, or Surya Namaskar. Join a sports club, take morning walks in a park, or simply dance to Bollywood music in your room.

7. Create New Goals and Memories

Shared memories make moving on difficult. Set new personal and professional goals. Work toward a promotion, explore new career paths, or challenge yourself with something meaningful that shifts your focus forward.

8. Change Your Environment

Consider moving to a new city or country for better opportunities. Solo travel is increasingly popular in India and can be deeply liberating. Reconnect with family members to maintain social bonds during this transition.

9. Learn New Skills

Use your time to learn something new—cooking, a new language, or a professional skill. These activities provide healthy distraction and foster a sense of achievement and confidence.

10. Lean on Your Support System

Despite their complexities, Indian families can be deeply comforting. If possible, talk to a parent, sibling, cousin, or trusted relative. Friends are equally important—form a support group if others are going through similar experiences. Knowing you’re not alone can be incredibly healing.

Seek Professional Help

If months have passed and you still struggle with daily functioning, severe depression, suicidal thoughts, or rely on substances or self-harm to cope, seek professional help immediately. Mental health professionals provide tools and support that family and friends cannot.

Qualified counselors on the BetterLyf platform can help individuals process past relationships and move forward with clarity and strength.

In Summary

Forgetting someone you loved is not about erasing them—it’s about reclaiming control over your present and future. Turn pain into wisdom, grief into growth, and a closed chapter into a lesson that shaped but did not define you.

You lost. And you will love again.
