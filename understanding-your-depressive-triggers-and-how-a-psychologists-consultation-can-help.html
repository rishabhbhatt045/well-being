Understanding Your Depressive Triggers and How a Psychologist’s Consultation Can Help

Depression is a mood disorder—a mental health concern that can result in persistent feelings of sadness and can adversely impact appetite, weight, sleep, and overall daily functioning. Depression is characterised by a marked loss of interest in pleasurable activities that we once enjoyed. It can affect cognition, perception, memory, and concentration, making everyday life feel overwhelming.

During difficult phases such as grieving the loss of a loved one, facing job loss, or navigating separation or divorce, it is natural to experience sadness and low mood. However, when these emotions persist for a prolonged period and are accompanied by additional symptoms, it may indicate clinical depression rather than temporary emotional distress.

According to the National Mental Health Survey (NHMS) 2015, approximately 5.3% of India’s population has experienced depressive disorders at some point in their lives—highlighting the need for awareness and timely psychological support.

Depression often develops due to a complex interplay of factors including environmental stressors, genetic predisposition, neurobiological changes, hormonal fluctuations, and traumatic life experiences. Physical trauma, such as brain injuries, may also significantly impact mental health. Let’s explore some of the most common depressive triggers.

Contributing Factors of Depressed Mood

1. Stress

Psychological stress affects both the mind and body. Chronic stress leads to the release of hormones like cortisol and adrenaline, which can disrupt emotional regulation. Stress can impair brain regions such as the hippocampus—commonly affected in individuals with depression—making it harder to regulate mood and emotions.

2. Grief

Losing a loved one—through death or the end of a meaningful relationship—can result in deep emotional pain. As grief progresses, the reality of loss may lead to intense sadness, withdrawal, anxiety, and depressive symptoms. Rumination often accompanies grief, making emotional recovery more difficult.

3. Rumination

Rumination involves repeatedly dwelling on negative thoughts, memories, or perceived failures. This pattern traps individuals in cycles of negativity, making it difficult to move toward problem-solving or emotional relief. Over time, rumination deepens depressive symptoms and reinforces hopelessness.

4. Rejection

Humans naturally seek connection and belonging. Experiencing rejection—whether socially, romantically, or professionally—can trigger sadness, anxiety, anger, and depressed mood. Repeated rejection may lead individuals to internalize feelings of unworthiness, resulting in withdrawal, isolation, and depression.

5. Chronic Illness

Living with chronic illness or long-term pain can significantly impact mental health. Diagnosis-related shock, physical discomfort, loss of independence, and medication side effects may contribute to depression. Depression, in turn, can worsen physical symptoms and slow recovery.

6. Career Struggles

Unemployment, job loss, workplace harassment, toxic work environments, micromanagement, and burnout can all contribute to emotional distress. Continuous career-related stress often leads to feelings of helplessness, anxiety, and depressive symptoms.

7. Financial Difficulties

Research suggests that financial strain and declining income increase the risk of depression. Financial stress can create a vicious cycle—depression makes it harder to address financial challenges, which further worsens mental health.

8. Body Image Concerns

Societal beauty standards and external judgment often lead to body image insecurities. When self-worth becomes closely tied to appearance, individuals may experience anxiety, low self-esteem, and depression.

9. Comorbid Conditions such as Substance Use

Depression and substance use often coexist and reinforce each other. Substances may be used as coping mechanisms to numb emotional pain, but they can worsen depressive symptoms. Integrated therapeutic treatment addressing both conditions improves recovery outcomes.

10. Loneliness

Prolonged loneliness and social isolation can both precede and result from depression. Feelings of emptiness, disconnection, and social anxiety may develop, making reintegration into social environments increasingly difficult.

Can Consulting a Psychologist Be Helpful?

Depression can be triggered by various emotional, psychological, and environmental factors. Gaining insight into these triggers is the first step toward healing. Consulting a psychologist—either online or offline—can help individuals understand their experiences, identify root causes, and develop healthy coping strategies.

Therapy for depression focuses on emotional regulation, mood stabilization, resilience-building, and restructuring negative thought patterns. Through depression counselling, a trained mental health professional helps individuals understand emotional triggers, challenge unhelpful thinking styles, and develop healthier coping mechanisms. This structured support encourages self-awareness, emotional balance, and long-term recovery by addressing both symptoms and underlying causes of depression.

Online counselling for depression—such as services offered by BetterLyf—provides accessible, confidential, and flexible support, helping individuals overcome geographical and time-related barriers. Therapy, when approached consistently, can be a powerful tool in managing depression and reclaiming emotional well-being.
